Delivery Plans

A practical guide to building, owning, and maintaining the TIDP — the discipline-level delivery plan that each Appointed Party owns, populates, and submits to form the Master Information Delivery Plan (MIDP).

ISO 19650-2 Per Discipline 2026 Edition
Definition
A Task Information Delivery Plan (TIDP) is a discipline-specific delivery schedule owned and maintained by each Appointed Party — listing every information container they are responsible for producing, with the format, suitability code, LOD, and submission milestone for each. Multiple TIDPs are consolidated by the Information Manager to form the Master Information Delivery Plan (MIDP).
Who?
Each Appointed Party
Every Appointed Party on the project — architect, structural engineer, MEP contractor, civil engineer — owns and maintains their own TIDP. It cannot be completed by the Information Manager on their behalf.
What?
Their Deliverables Only
The TIDP covers only the deliverables within the scope of that Appointed Party — models, drawings, documents, and reports that they are contractually responsible for producing.
When?
Their Task Schedule
Submission dates are defined by the Appointed Party based on their internal production programme — but must align with the MIDP milestones and stage gates agreed in the BEP.
Feeds?
The MIDP
All TIDPs are submitted to the Information Manager and consolidated into the MIDP. Without a complete and accurate TIDP from every Appointed Party, the MIDP cannot be finalised.
Key Distinction
TIDP vs MIDP — Understanding the Difference
TIDP — Task Information Delivery Plan
Discipline-Level — One per Appointed Party
Owned by each individual Appointed Party
Covers only that party's deliverables
Authored by the discipline team lead
Submitted to Info Manager for consolidation
Updated whenever scope or programme changes
Input document — feeds the MIDP
MIDP — Master Information Delivery Plan
Project-Level — One per Project
Owned by the Information Manager
Covers all deliverables across all disciplines
Compiled from all submitted TIDPs
Issued to client and all Appointed Parties
Updated at each stage gate
Output document — the master schedule

Common Errors
5 TIDP Mistakes to Avoid
1
Info Manager completing TIDP on party's behalf
The TIDP must be completed by the Appointed Party who owns the deliverables. An Information Manager who fills it in on their behalf creates a schedule the authoring team has not validated — and will not own.
2
Milestones copied from MIDP without checking capacity
Copying MIDP dates directly into the TIDP without checking whether the authoring team can actually meet them creates false confidence. TIDP dates must reflect real production capacity.
3
Deliverables missing from TIDP scope
If a deliverable is not in the TIDP, it will not appear in the MIDP — and will therefore have no delivery date, no suitability code, and no CDE submission path. Everything must be listed, however minor.
4
TIDP submitted once and never updated
The TIDP is a live schedule. Scope changes, design evolution, and programme revisions all require TIDP updates. A static TIDP that diverges from actual production quickly becomes meaningless as a delivery tool.
5
Suitability codes left blank or defaulted to S4
Every deliverable must have a suitability code defined per stage. Defaulting everything to S4 bypasses the controlled review process — CDE workflows will fail and the client will lose trust in the delivery process.

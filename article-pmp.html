Definition
The Project Management Plan (PMP) for BIM is a governance document that defines how the project will be managed from a digital delivery perspective — covering scope boundaries, risk identification and mitigation, change control procedures, communication protocols, and stakeholder reporting frameworks. It aligns BIM delivery with contractual obligations and programme milestones.
Scope
What is Included
Defines the precise boundaries of the BIM scope — what models, deliverables, and services are included and what is explicitly excluded — eliminating scope creep from day one.
Risk
What Could Go Wrong
Identifies, assesses, and mitigates project risks that could impact BIM delivery — from CDE failures and software incompatibilities to resourcing gaps and data security threats.
Change
How Changes Are Managed
Defines the formal change control process — how scope changes are requested, assessed, approved, and communicated — preventing unauthorised model changes from destabilising delivery.
Comms
How We Report
Establishes stakeholder communication cadence, reporting format, escalation paths, and the meeting structure for BIM coordination and progress review throughout the project.
Structure
6 Core Sections of the BIM Project Management Plan
📋
Scope Management
Defines what is in and out of BIM scope — deliverables, disciplines, stages, and services. Includes scope change triggers and approval thresholds.
⚠️
Risk Management
Live risk register covering probability, impact, mitigation, and ownership for all BIM-related project risks across the delivery lifecycle.
🔄
Change Control
Formal process for raising, assessing, approving, and implementing scope, programme, or contractual changes that affect BIM delivery.
📡
Communication Plan
Meeting schedule, reporting cadence, escalation paths, and stakeholder matrix — defining who receives what information and how often.
📅
Programme Alignment
Maps BIM milestones to the master programme — ensuring MIDP dates, stage gates, and information exchanges are integrated with the construction programme.
📊
Performance Reporting
KPIs, dashboards, and progress metrics used to report BIM delivery health to the client, project manager, and senior stakeholders.

Change Control
BIM Change Control Process — Step by Step
01
Identify
Change identified by any party — scope, programme, data requirement or contractual
02
Request
Formal Change Request (CR) raised with description, impact, and priority
03
Assess
Info Manager & PM assess BIM impact — cost, programme, resource, model
04
Approve
Client and PM sign off. Threshold defined — minor changes delegated to Info Mgr
05
Implement
BEP, MIDP, MPDT updated. Models revised. CDE notified. Programme adjusted.
06
Close
Change confirmed implemented. CR closed in register. All parties notified.

Common Errors
5 PMP Mistakes to Avoid
1
Scope not defined — only deliverables listed
Listing deliverables without defining scope boundaries leaves the door open for unlimited scope creep. The PMP must explicitly state what is out of scope — not just what is in.
2
No formal change control process enforced
Informal verbal scope changes are the single largest source of BIM delivery failure. Every scope change — however minor — must go through the formal change request process before any work begins.
3
Risk register created at mobilisation and never updated
A risk register that is not updated is not a risk management tool — it is a filing exercise. Risks must be reviewed monthly and updated as project conditions change throughout the lifecycle.
4
BIM PMP disconnected from the master PMP
The BIM PMP must align with the overarching project PMP — sharing the same programme milestones, escalation paths, and reporting structure. A BIM PMP that operates in isolation creates contradictory governance.
5
No communication plan for BIM stakeholders
Without a defined communication plan, BIM progress reports are issued ad hoc, escalations are missed, and client expectations diverge from delivery reality. Meeting cadence and reporting format must be agreed at mobilisation.
